A causal algorithm inferred the causes of child PTSD from data no one could randomize

Run over 1,354 maltreated children in a decades-old cohort, causal discovery named four direct causes of posttraumatic stress at age 8, and the same run shows which of them a second study should expect to lose.

Posttraumatic stress in young children at risk for maltreatment: a causal data science analysis

Saxe, Morales, Ma, Urgurbil, Aliferis · Frontiers in Health Services 6:1784459 · 2026

Read the paper
This article features the application of Causal Data Science (CDS) methods to determine the mechanism for Posttraumatic Stress (PTS) in young, maltreated children, in order to advance knowledge for prevention. Advances in prevention require research that identifies causal factors, but the scientific literature that would inform the identification of causes are almost exclusively based on the application of correlational methods to observational data. Causal inferences from such research will frequently be in error. We conducted the present study to explore the application of CDS methods as an alternative—or a supplement—to experimental methods, which can rarely be applied in human research on causal factors for PTS. A data processing pipeline that integrates state-of-the-art CDS algorithms was applied to an existing observational, longitudinal data set collected by the Consortium for Longitudinal Studies in Child Abuse and Neglect (LONGSCAN). This data set contains a sample of 1,354 children who were identified in infancy to early childhood as being maltreated or at risk. A causal network model of 251 variables (nodes) and 818 bivariate relations (edges) was discovered, revealing four direct causes (Emotional Maltreatment at age 0–4, Physical Assault at age 8, Feeling of Safety at age 8, and Witnessing Violence at age 8) and two direct effects (Negative Self-Image and Severe Assault from a Non-Caregiver at age 8) of PTS at age 8, within a network containing a broad diversity of causal pathways. These results indicate that CDS methods show promise for research on the complex etiology of PTS in young, maltreated children.1

Before you read

The paper stands on two bodies of knowledge and assumes you hold neither. From machine learning it takes constraint-based causal discovery: the family of algorithms that reads a causal graph off the pattern of conditional independences in data, the PC algorithm and its relatives, colliders, d-separation, the Markov blanket. Those you may already carry, and the piece leans on them. From clinical psychology it takes something you almost certainly do not carry: what posttraumatic stress is in a child too young to describe it, how it gets measured at that age, and what a cohort of children "at risk for maltreatment" actually is. Everything clinical is built here before it is spent.

The cause you are not allowed to test

To prove a cause, randomize it. Split a population in two, change one thing for one half, and any difference that follows is the thing you changed. This is the engine under every clinical trial, and it is unavailable for almost every question that matters about child maltreatment. You cannot assign children to be abused. So the field that most needs to know what causes lasting harm is confined to watching harm happen and measuring what travels with it, which is a way of learning correlations and calling them causes when the paper's discussion turns to prevention. The authors name this predicament plainly: the literature meant to identify causes is "almost exclusively based on the application of correlational methods to observational data," and "causal inferences from such research will frequently be in error."1

Across trauma-exposed children, the pooled rate of PTSD is about 16%, rising to roughly a third of girls after interpersonal trauma of the kind maltreatment inflicts.2 The paper cites a still higher band, 30 to 40%, for maltreated children specifically.1 A prevention program has to know which levers, pulled early, actually bend that number, and correlation cannot tell it apart from the many things that merely ride along. The paper's wager is that a class of algorithms built for exactly this gap, causal discovery, can extract defensible causal structure from data that was only ever observed. It is a wager worth watching precisely because it is so hard to win.

Who the 1,354 children are, and what "PTS" means at age 8

The data was not collected for this study. It comes from LONGSCAN, a consortium begun in 1991 that followed children who were maltreated or at elevated risk of it, across five United States sites, from age 4 toward adulthood, interviewing child and caregiver separately at ages 4, 6, 8, and beyond, and pulling child-protective-services records every two years.3 Two features of that design shape everything downstream. First, "at risk for maltreatment" is an enrollment criterion, not a per-child verdict: the sites ranged from low-income community samples to children already in foster care, so risk is a property of how the cohort was recruited, not a diagnosis each child carries.3 Second, the wave structure at 4, 6, and 8 gives the analysis its one firm handhold on time, which will matter greatly when the algorithm has to decide which way a causal arrow points.

Of the 1,354 children, 1,053 had the outcome the study is about, so the analysis runs on them.1 That outcome needs care, because "PTSD in an eight-year-old" is not the adult disorder shrunk down. A young child cannot narrate flashbacks or report a foreshortened sense of the future, and DSM-5 acknowledged this by adding a developmental subtype, posttraumatic stress disorder in children six and younger, the first developmental subtype of any DSM disorder. It lowers the threshold for the avoidance-and-numbing cluster from three symptoms to one and drops the criteria that require a child to verbalize internal states, such as persistent negative beliefs about oneself.4 The practical consequence is that trauma stress at this age is read from behavior a caregiver can see, not from what a child can say.

So the study does not measure a diagnosis at all. Its target is a posttraumatic-stress T-score from the Trauma Symptom Checklist for Young Children, a 90-item instrument on which a caregiver rates how often each symptom has appeared over the past month.5 A T-score is normed to a mean of 50 and a standard deviation of 10; the sample averages 52.92, a shade above the norm.1 The outcome, then, is a caregiver's rating of a child's observable symptoms, not a clinician's diagnosis and not the child's own account. Every causal claim the paper makes terminates here, and the claim is only ever as good as the measurement it lands on.

How an algorithm points a causal arrow

Causal discovery starts from a fact any statistician knows and inverts it. Correlation does not imply causation, but causation leaves a specific fingerprint in the correlations. If A causes B causes C, then A and C are dependent, yet once you condition on B they come apart: B screens them off. Read enough of these conditional independences and you can reconstruct much of the graph that produced them. The canonical method is the PC algorithm, which first deletes an edge between any two variables that turn out conditionally independent given some set of others, leaving a skeleton, then orients edges by hunting for colliders, the pattern where two arrows collide into one variable without the parents being adjacent.6

Markov boundary The smallest set of variables that makes the target independent of everything else. Under the method's assumptions it is exactly the target's direct causes, its direct effects, and the other direct causes of those effects. It is the local causal neighborhood, and it is what the paper reports for PTS.

This buys power at the price of assumptions, and the assumptions are where a reader's scrutiny belongs. The method needs faithfulness: every conditional independence in the data must reflect the true graph's structure rather than two effects happening to cancel. The authors reassure that unfaithful distributions are vanishingly rare, "Lebesque measure 0" in their phrase, which is true in theory and says nothing about whether a finite, noisy sample of 1,053 children behaves.1 It also needs the graph to be complete enough that no unmeasured variable is secretly a common cause of two it does see. And even with both, observation alone cannot fully orient a graph: it can only pin it down to a Markov equivalence class, the set of graphs that share the same skeleton and colliders and are otherwise indistinguishable from the data.1

Time is how the study escapes the tie. Because a cause cannot follow its effect, the eight developmental époques from constitutional and perinatal factors through the age-8 wave supply orientations no statistics could. In the final network, 35.5% of the 818 edges were pointed by time ordering alone and 59.3% by the algorithm's collider rules, leaving 43 edges the method could not orient.1 The pipeline itself, GLL-PC, extends this constraint-based logic into a framework for learning the local structure around one target variable in high dimensions from modest samples, work co-authored by the paper's senior author.7 Applied to the PTS T-score, it returns that target's Markov boundary: 19 variables, 28 edges.1

Four things that make it, two things it makes

Inside that boundary sit four direct causes of posttraumatic stress at age 8: emotional maltreatment recorded between ages 0 and 4, physical assault at 8, witnessing violence at 8, and the child's feeling of safety at 8.1 Three of the four are unsurprising in direction and valuable in status. That witnessing violence and being assaulted raise trauma symptoms is not news; that an algorithm designed to strip out confounding still finds the edge, after conditioning on a broad field of alternatives, is what the correlational literature could never establish. The fourth cause is the interesting one. Feeling safe is not an exposure done to the child but a state inside it, and the model places it as a direct cause rather than a byproduct, which is what makes it a candidate prevention lever rather than a symptom to wait out.

The two direct effects run the other way, out of PTS. The first, negative self-image, is the expected downstream cost, and it is worth pausing on: the developmental criteria deliberately de-emphasize self-referential beliefs at younger ages because children cannot yet articulate them,4 so seeing the construct emerge as a consequence by age 8 tracks a real developmental window opening. The second effect is the one that stops a reader, because it seems to run backward.

The arrow that points from stress to more harm

The model says PTS at age 8 is a direct cause of severe assault by a non-caregiver at age 8. A trauma symptom causing a later assault inverts the usual reading, in which assault causes trauma. The authors do not dodge it; they propose a mechanism. A child in distress reaches for protection, and when the caregivers who should provide it are absent, incapacitated, or themselves the source of harm, the child's reaching turns toward adults outside any caregiving role, where the risk of exploitation is highest.1 On that reading the arrow is not backward at all. Distress produces a behavior, help-seeking under conditions of caregiver failure, and the behavior raises exposure to a specific danger. The direction is not invented for this dataset: a systematic review of childhood maltreatment and revictimization finds prospective evidence that PTSD symptom severity mediates the path from earlier victimization to later victimization, though it rates that evidence as preliminary.8

This is the single best thing in the paper for a reader learning how causal discovery earns its keep, and also the single most fragile claim, and the two facts are the same fact. An edge this surprising is exactly what a correlational study would either miss or dismiss as reverse causation. It is also exactly the kind of edge that a time-ordering assumption within a single wave cannot firmly orient, and that the authors flag as sensitive to sampling variation, calling for replication before anyone builds on it.1 The right response is neither to discard it nor to believe it, but to treat it as the model's most testable prediction: a hypothesis sharp enough to design the next study around.

What the run licenses, and what it does not

The paper's own honesty is its best feature, and reading it well means reproducing that honesty rather than the abstract's cleaner headline. Take the confident sentence, "four direct causes," against the buried concession that "edges between several variables were observed in less than 30% of bootstrapped samples."1 Bootstrapping refits the model on 100 resamples of the children and asks how often each edge survives. An edge that appears in under a third of them is not thereby false, and the authors are right that low power at this sample size can hide a true edge. But it is a signal that the edge may be an artifact of these particular 1,053 children, and the reader should carry the four causes with the stability written next to each, not the flat confidence of the summary.

What holds up
  • Edges that echo the trauma literature (witnessing violence, physical assault, feeling of safety) now carry a causal reading the correlational work could not supply, unconfounded by the 251 other measured variables.
  • A multiplicity test confirmed a single Markov boundary for PTS, which is the internal consistency check the method demands.
  • The design converts an untestable question into a set of concrete, falsifiable pathways a prevention study can target.
What to be careful about
  • The outcome is a caregiver-reported symptom T-score, not a diagnosis; a shared reporter can couple causes and effect in ways the graph reads as structure.
  • Faithfulness and, above all, no unmeasured confounding are assumptions, not results. The authors concede the method "cannot rule out the possibility of confounding from unmeasured variables."1
  • Several edges, including the counterintuitive one, are unstable under resampling and await replication in a second cohort.

One smaller thing the reader can check without the data: the paper reports 43 of 818 edges left unoriented and calls that 6.3%, but 43 of 818 is 5.3%. The slip is harmless to the argument and useful as a reminder that a network with 818 edges is still assembled by hand and eye at the edges, and deserves the arithmetic double-check any result does.1

The honest verdict is that this is a promising method used carefully on a hard problem, and that its value is a menu of prevention hypotheses rather than a settled etiology. The authors say as much when they list the levers with the broadest reach across the 138 pathways, helping a child feel safer, treating a caregiver's partner's lack of empathy, and supporting a four-year-old's motor development, each an intervention someone could actually run and test.1 What causal discovery adds here is not certainty about the causes of child PTSD. It is a principled way to decide which of the many things that travel with it are worth the expense of trying to change, on data where the experiment can never be run. That is a real contribution, and it is a smaller one than "the causes were found," which is the reading the paper takes pains to refuse.
