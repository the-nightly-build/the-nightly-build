Was the United States Right to Decline a 16-Year USMCA Renewal?

By refusing to extend USMCA to 2036 in favor of yearly reviews, the Trump administration wagered leverage against certainty, and serious people disagree about which matters more.

8 min read2026-07-11

The renewal Washington left on the table

On July 1, 2026, the trade ministers of the United States, Mexico, and Canada met to conduct the joint review that the region's trade pact requires at its sixth anniversary. Two of the three governments wanted to lock the deal in for another decade. The United States did not. In the words of U.S. Trade Representative Jamieson Greer, the country "did not agree to renew the USMCA in its current form," and would instead keep working with its neighbors "to address the Agreement's shortcomings and our trade deficits with these countries."1

The joint review Under Article 34.7 the pact runs a 16-year term to July 1, 2036, with a review at year six. If all three governments confirm the extension, the clock resets for another 16 years. If any one declines, the deal does not die: it stays fully in force, and the three parties instead meet in a review every year until they either agree to extend or reach the 2036 sunset.

That mechanism is why the decision is less dramatic than "the deal is dead" and more consequential than a missed deadline. Nothing changed for a shipment crossing the border on July 2. Current tariff preferences, rules of origin, and investment protections all continue unaffected, and the parties can still confirm the long extension in writing at any point before 2036.2 What changed is the horizon. In place of a settled decade, North America now has an agreement reviewed annually against a growing list of American grievances: persistent goods-trade deficits with both neighbors, Chinese goods routed through Mexico to dodge U.S. tariffs, blocked access for U.S. dairy in Canada, and state dominance of Mexico's energy sector.3 The stakes are large because the three economies are stitched together: a single component can cross a North American border several times before it is finished, so the terms of that crossing shape factory-siting decisions that outlast any one review.

The question dividing serious trade thinkers is not whether those grievances are real. It is whether declining the renewal was the right way to press them. One camp reads the annual review as leverage preserved: the United States kept its hand on the lever instead of signing away the one moment of maximum pressure. The other reads it as certainty forfeited: the value of a trade pact is the multi-decade predictability it buys, and yearly reviews spend exactly that.

The Trump administration and trade-balance hawks

A clean 16-year renewal would have locked in a flawed deal and surrendered the leverage to fix it; annual reviews keep the partners at the table while the pact stays in force.

The starting premise is that the current agreement underdelivers for the United States and that renewal should be earned, not automatic. By one law-firm account of the review, Greer said Washington would not "rubber stamp" the deal, citing "substantial issues" with its present form.4 The substance behind that posture is a view of trade that treats balance, not volume, as the measure of success. Oren Cass, chief economist of American Compass and a former Manhattan Institute senior fellow who testifies to Congress on trade,5 argues that the United States "run[s] large structural trade deficits now above a trillion dollars a year," and that while trade can benefit both sides, "it does have to be balanced."6

On that reading, the joint review is precisely the inflection point at which to demand better terms. Cass has written that the pact "provides the basic framework for trading relationships" in North America, and that its renegotiation is "an obvious inflection point for the American approach to economic alliances," at which partners should be pressed to adopt common tariff barriers, investment restrictions, and export controls that keep Chinese goods out of the bloc.7 A 16-year extension, signed now, would have foreclosed that demand until 2032. Declining it keeps the deal operating with no cliff for business while resetting the calendar to an annual meeting where the same leverage recurs, the mechanism analysts describe as serial reviews that preserve negotiating pressure over transshipment, energy, and market access.3 The transshipment worry is concrete: without tighter rules on non-market inputs, the argument runs, a renewed pact would let goods with substantial Chinese content enter the United States duty-free through Mexican assembly, converting a North American deal into a back door for the exact trade the tariffs were meant to stop. A standing annual review is the instrument for writing and enforcing those disciplines, one the signatories would lose the moment they confirmed a clean sixteen-year term.

Free-market economists and North American manufacturers

The certainty of a decade-long renewal is the product itself, and trading it for annual reviews injects the very uncertainty that deters the long-horizon investment the policy claims to want.

This side does not dispute that the pact matters; it argues that its value is inseparable from its durability. Scott Lincicome, a trade economist at the Cato Institute writing in Bloomberg Opinion, notes that trilateral trade in goods and services reached $1.99 trillion in 2024 and regional foreign direct investment nearly $380 billion, and that "roughly 71% of US imports from Canada and 64% from Mexico are industrial inputs" feeding American factories rather than finished consumer goods.8 A deal that binds that machine is worth keeping. His objection is to the timing signal, not the trade: a decade-long expiration window, he writes, "introduces new and significant uncertainty for global businesses that make large-scale expansion plans on multi-decade timelines."8

The concern is that the uncertainty is itself a cost, borne before any concession is won. Tax Foundation economists estimate that if the exemptions eventually lapsed and tariffs snapped back, the effect would be a $466 billion tax increase over 2027 to 2036, about $300 per household in the first year, and 95,000 fewer full-time-equivalent jobs; even short of that, they write, "a prolonged review process would heighten trade policy uncertainty, which is already elevated."9 Forecasters watching corporate behavior report the chilling directly. "We may get mandatory annual reviews, but that also means that uncertainty prevails, and that's a negative for decision-making for businesses," Oxford Economics' Tony Stillo told Al Jazeera before the review.10 The mechanism is straightforward. A factory or a supplier contract is a bet on the rules holding for years; when those rules are reopened every twelve months, the rational move is to defer the bet or hedge it elsewhere, and that deferral is a cost the United States pays whether or not any concession is ever won. The reshoring the administration says it wants, on this account, depends on the predictability it just declined to grant.

Scott Lincicome, Cato Institute, standing here as a trade economist who has studied USMCA closely and argued in print that the pact is "simply too important to blow up," even as he judges the missed renewal a self-inflicted cost.8

Whether leverage buys more than uncertainty costs

Strip the rhetoric and the two sides agree on nearly all the facts. The deal stays in force to 2036; some $2 trillion in annual trade continues to flow; the review is now yearly rather than settled. They even share a diagnosis that the status quo has irritants worth fixing. The real disagreement is a forecast, and beneath it an unsettled empirical question. The forecast: will an annual review actually extract better terms on deficits, transshipment, dairy, and energy, or will it mostly impose a standing discount on North American investment for a decade? The empirical question underneath it is whether bilateral goods deficits are the problem the leverage case takes them to be. The same CSIS analysis that catalogues the U.S. grievances also notes that the imbalances with the neighbors are modest by the administration's own standard: in 2025 the United States imported $1.58 from Mexico and $1.14 from Canada for every dollar it exported, against $12.40 from Vietnam.3 If that ratio reads as ordinary coproduction, the leverage has less to buy; if it reads as extraction, the review is the tool to correct it.

What would move a fair reader is evidence that is not yet in, and it should begin to arrive on a knowable schedule. The first annual review falls around July 2027; its outcome, whether Washington converts the preserved leverage into concrete concessions or the meeting passes with communiqués and no deal, is the leverage case's first real test.11 Running against it, over the next twelve to eighteen months, are the investment figures: the $380 billion regional FDI baseline Lincicome cites is the number to watch for the chill his side predicts,8 and analysts have framed the coming period as the pact staying in force "but under a cloud of sustained uncertainty that discourages long-term investment bets on North America."3 A further complication both sides flag is that any renegotiated terms may need congressional approval, which neither the leverage nor the certainty case fully controls.11 Concessions banked against investment lost: that ledger, not this week's statements, is what will show whether declining the renewal was the shrewder move or the costlier one. The timing is unusually legible for a policy dispute, because the review recurs on a fixed date and the investment data is reported quarterly, so a reader need not settle the question on faith. Within roughly a year and a half, the first evidence on both halves of the wager should be on the table.
