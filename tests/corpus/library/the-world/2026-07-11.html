A caught rocket, a record-hot June, and Russia's fuel squeeze

China nets an orbital booster and Europe logs its hottest June while Ukrainian strikes ration Russia's petrol and Ebola spreads unseen in Congo.

4 min read2026-07-11
wildcard

China catches a Long March 10B first stage in a sea-borne net1

On July 10 the Long March 10B lifted off from Hainan and, about six minutes after separation, lowered its first stage under power onto a tensioned net aboard a recovery ship, snagged by landing hooks rather than legs. State contractor CASC called it the first net-system recovery of a carrier rocket and said it aims to refly the booster before year's end; the reusable stage is rated for 16,000 kg to low orbit.

Why it matters: reuse is the lever that cut launch cost for SpaceX, and China is now the second country to recover an orbital-class booster. The hook-and-net design avoids the mass penalty of landing legs, a bet that only pays off once the same stage flies twice.

health & science

Western Europe logs its hottest June on record2

Western Europe averaged 20.74°C in June, 3.05°C above the 1991–2020 norm and past its own 2025 record, per the Copernicus Climate Change Service. Globally it was the second-warmest June, while the extra-polar ocean set a June sea-surface record; Barcelona's Fabra Observatory hit 40.5°C on July 8, its highest in more than a century.

Why it matters: back-to-back June records, paired with the warmest ocean surface, mark a heat baseline that is shifting rather than spiking. Sustained ocean warmth loads the atmosphere with the energy that drives the summer's heatwaves and storms.

world

Russia rations petrol as strikes cripple its refineries3

Ukrainian drone strikes have disabled roughly a quarter of Russia's oil-refining capacity, one Russian energy analyst estimates, forcing stations in hard-hit regions to cap sales near 20 to 30 litres per vehicle. Moscow now plans to import about 400,000 tonnes of petrol a month, including 60,000 to 80,000 tonnes from India.

Why it matters: a top crude exporter importing gasoline is a telling reversal. Refineries are fixed, high-value targets that are slow to repair, so the strikes convert Ukraine's drones into a durable tax on Russia's war economy.

economy & finance

SK Hynix's $26.5bn New York debut jumps 13%4

The Korean memory-chip maker's $26.5bn listing, the largest foreign IPO to date, rose 13% on July 10 as the S&P 500 closed at 7,575.39, near its record. The 10-year Treasury yield sat at 4.56%, an uncommon pairing of climbing borrowing costs with record equities.

Why it matters: appetite for the high-bandwidth memory that feeds AI accelerators drew the biggest foreign debut on record. Stocks pressing highs against a rising long yield is the market betting on growth even as it prices money as no longer cheap.

policy & law

Supreme Court lets Texas's app-store age-verification law stand5

On July 7 the justices denied, without noted dissent, emergency applications from a tech trade group and a student advocacy group to block Texas's SB 2420, which requires app stores to verify users' ages and secure parental consent before minors download apps. The unsigned order leaves the law enforceable while the First Amendment challenge proceeds; it did not rule on the merits.

Why it matters: this is the first app-store age law to clear the Court, shifting the verification burden onto Apple and Google at the platform layer. Other states have copied the Texas text, so the order clears the runway for a national patchwork before the constitutional question is settled.

world

India and New Zealand elevate ties to a strategic partnership6

Meeting in Auckland on the first visit by an Indian prime minister in 40 years, Narendra Modi and Christopher Luxon upgraded relations to a strategic partnership spanning defence and naval exercises, trade, agri-tech and science, and set a goal of doubling bilateral trade by 2030. The visit followed a Chinese ballistic-missile test into the Pacific days earlier.

Why it matters: it is another Indo-Pacific hedge against China's reach, knitting a Pacific state into India's security and technology orbit. The four-decade gap between visits shows how far the region's alignments have moved.

health & science

DR Congo's Ebola toll passes 500, with no vaccine for the strain7

The WHO's country representative reported more than 500 deaths among over 1,500 confirmed cases in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, an outbreak spreading largely undetected. It is caused by the Bundibugyo species, for which no vaccine or specific treatment is licensed, and the case-fatality ratio runs near 31%.8

Why it matters: the licensed Ebola vaccine targets the Zaire species, not this one, so containment falls back on tracing and isolation as cases reach new provinces and one infection has already been exported to France. The gap is a reminder that countermeasures are pathogen-specific, not generic.
