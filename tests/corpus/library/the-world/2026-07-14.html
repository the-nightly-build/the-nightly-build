Iran strikes two UAE oil tankers in Hormuz

Washington's threat to toll a strait it does not own turns a military standoff into an economic one and carries Brent past $85.

5 min read2026-07-14
world

Two Iranian missiles hit UAE tankers, killing a crewman1

Two Iranian cruise missiles struck the UAE supertankers Mombasa and Al Bahiyah in the southern shipping lane of the Strait of Hormuz early on Tuesday, killing one crew member, an Indian national aboard the Mombasa, and wounding eight others, four of them seriously, according to the UAE defence ministry. The strike came as US Central Command pressed a bombing campaign it resumed on 7 July and now says has hit more than 300 targets across Iran.2 Trump has told Congress the resumption restarted a 60-day war-powers clock his administration earlier argued had lapsed after April's ceasefire.

Why it matters: Iran cannot match American airpower, so it is answering where it holds an edge: the shipping lane the strikes are meant to keep open. A single missile against a tanker unsettles the oil market Washington is trying to calm, at a fraction of the cost of a night of bombing. That asymmetry is why pressure on crude is likely to persist even if the airstrikes taper.

economy & finance

Trump threatens a 20% toll on Hormuz shipping3

Brent crude climbed to about $85.77 a barrel on Tuesday, up roughly 3% on the day and more than 10% on the week, after Trump said Washington would blockade Iranian ports and charge a 20% fee on all other cargo crossing the Strait of Hormuz. The disruption is registering as a seaborne transit premium: in the same session Brent, the waterborne benchmark, traded about $5 above WTI, the landlocked US grade ($84.72 against $79.57), because a toll or blockade in Hormuz threatens oil that must move by ship, not the crude piped and railed across North America.4

Why it matters: a war premium built on transit risk unwinds the moment ships sail safely, unlike one built on barrels that no longer exist. That $5 gap says the market is pricing a blocked route with the fields behind it still intact, so the price can fall as fast as it rose once the strait clears, and stay elevated only as long as the missiles fly.

policy & law

Twelve states sue to block the $111bn Paramount-Warner deal5

A dozen states, led by California attorney general Rob Bonta, filed suit on Monday in the Northern District of California to block Paramount's $111 billion takeover of Warner Bros. Discovery, arguing it violates Section 7 of the Clayton Act. The complaint says the merger would “extinguish competition between Paramount and Warner Bros. and inflict substantial harm on movie theatres, basic cable distributors, and, ultimately, audiences nationwide.” The filing lands after the Justice Department cleared the deal in June, pitting state enforcers against a federal government that signed off.

Why it matters: the Justice Department's approval no longer settles a deal this size. When a dozen states can drag an already-cleared merger before a judge, the practical veto over media consolidation moves to whichever attorneys general choose to act, and this filing shows they will.

health & science

A THC-CBD drug eases dementia agitation in a Phase II trial6

A cannabis-derived formulation combining THC and CBD reduced agitation significantly more than placebo in hospice-eligible dementia patients, researchers reported on Tuesday at the Alzheimer's Association International Conference in London. In the LiBBY trial, a randomized, double-blind Phase II study of 120 participants across 10 medical centres with visits run in participants' homes, close to 90% of those treated showed overall improvement after 12 weeks. As a Phase II result it establishes a signal, not a licence: the finding still needs a larger confirmatory trial before the combination could reach the clinic.

Why it matters: a 90% improvement rate in a symptom clinicians can mostly only manage is a strong signal, but a 120-person trial still only measures promise a larger study has to confirm. If a confirmatory study holds the result, the value is as much in the method as the molecule: a treatment tested and delivered at home could reach the hospice patients that hospital-based trials routinely miss.

health & science

Melting in the North Pacific can stall an Atlantic current7

Meltwater from the North Pacific can, on its own, weaken the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation, the conveyor that carries warm water toward Europe, a UC Davis team reported on Monday in Nature Communications. Studying the last ice age, the researchers found that North Pacific iceberg discharge could have set off the abrupt cold spells known as Heinrich stadials, events long blamed on meltwater from the North Atlantic alone. The AMOC moves roughly 70% of the ocean's heat across the equator, so where its freshwater brake begins is not a technicality.

Why it matters: the result is about the deep past, not a forecast for this decade, and it does not say the current is failing now. What it changes is the map of what could weaken it: if a distant ocean's meltwater can reach across the planet to slow the Atlantic, models that watch only Greenland for the next disruption are watching too small a patch.

wildcard

A printed contact lens, made to fit in 20 minutes8

Researchers at the University of Waterloo have 3D-printed hard contact lenses matched to an individual eye in about 20 minutes, using a new hydrophilic silicone chemistry, and propose them as a way to deliver medication to the eye in place of drops. The appeal is customization at speed: a lens printed on the spot and shaped to a single eye, where stock lenses come in fixed sizes. For now the work is preliminary, with biocompatibility shown in the lab but no test yet in a living eye.

Why it matters: printing a device to fit in the time of an eye exam turns a stock commodity into a per-patient product, and pairing that with drug delivery could help people who forget or fumble their drops. The caution is stage: biocompatibility in a dish is a long way from safety in an eye, and the team has run no trial in one.
