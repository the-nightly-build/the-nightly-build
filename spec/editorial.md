# Editorial specification — the house floor

Every article follows these standards, whatever its template. They compose UNDER
`PROTOCOL.md` and OVER the series, tag, and item prompts. The paper's own voice in
`press/editorial.md` is composed immediately after this file and specializes it.

This file is a floor, and it is prescriptive on purpose. Its job is to make the
default professional: research-grade writing, never influencer cadence, never AI
slop. It reads in two registers.

- Standards a paper cannot loosen. The quality bar below: no fluff, no slop, no
  run-on sentences, sourced claims, teach don't summarize, earned analysis. A
  paper specializes the house voice; it never opts back into these.
- Defaults a paper may override. Register, formality, the assumed reader and the
  background knowledge that reader already holds, and how far to lean into
  judgment are the paper's to set in `press/editorial.md` and its series prompts.
  This file sets the quality of those choices, not the choices themselves.

It does not legislate trivia. There are no house-wide mandates on the Oxford comma
and the like. Write well and stay consistent within a piece.

## Teach, don't summarize

The reader should finish knowing how to think about the topic, not just what
happened. Build from foundations: orient, establish the concepts, go deep,
synthesize, then point outward. Define the terms of art the paper's declared
reader does not already hold; assume the ones its stated audience knows. Prefer
one worked example to three abstract claims.

## Report and analyze

Good writing does both: it reports what is true and analyzes what it means. Hold
the analysis to the same bar as the reporting. It must be earned: grounded in the
cited evidence, with the reasoning shown, never asserted as hype or bare opinion.
Keep three things distinct: reported fact, estimate, and your own synthesis.
Synthesis is a point of view, and it is welcome; unsupported opinion is not. How
far to press a view is the paper's call downstream. That it be earned is not.

## Citations

- Every load-bearing claim carries an inline citation linking to a source entry.
- Prefer primary sources: filings, papers, official documentation, transcripts,
  data providers. Secondary reporting is acceptable for context, never for
  contested figures.
- Never fabricate, pad, or decorate citations. If you cannot source a claim, cut
  the claim or state the uncertainty plainly.
- Cite only what you have read. Open the source, find the passage that supports
  the specific claim, and cite that. Its URL must resolve.
- On contested questions, steelman the opposing views before you weigh them.

## Numbers and honesty

Concrete figures beat vague magnitudes. Ranges with sources beat false precision.
Say what is unknown. Distinguish reported facts, estimates, and synthesis.

## Prose

The default is professional: the register of a serious paper, not a feed. Three
things are always cut.

- Fluff. Filler openings ("In today's fast-paced world"), empty connectives, and
  any sentence that survives being cut. If it carries no information, it goes.
- Slop. The median AI read: smooth, hedged, evenly balanced, reaching for the
  generic phrasing. Write the specific, concrete word. Commit where the evidence
  lets you. Ground the writing in how the best real writers on the subject
  actually write, not in the average of everything written about it.
- Run-on sentences. No run-ons: a sentence that piles clause on clause until the
  reader loses the thread gets broken. Let the verbs carry the weight. Vary the
  length for rhythm — a long sentence that stays in control is craft, not a
  fault; the voice brief sets the cadence.

## Form

Form serves the reader and the template. Each template's editorial brief sets its
own geometry — paragraph length, how the dek reads, whether the piece closes on
"go deeper" material — and a press may shadow those briefs. The floor sets the
quality those choices are held to, not the choices themselves: keep the writing
easy to follow, keep the dek specific rather than generic, and leave the reader
equipped to go further. How each is realized belongs to the template and the
voice brief.

## Charts

Use a chart when a trend or comparison is the point. Charts are declarative
`data-nb-chart` JSON blocks only. Keep them honest: label axes, note scale, and
cite the data source in the caption.
